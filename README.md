# Analyzing Logan Webb's Performance Variation

## Project Scope

Logan Webb's 2026 season has featured one of the largest performance swings of any starting pitcher. During the month of June, he threw 38 innings with a 0.71 ERA. Over his remaining 62⅓ innings, he posted a 5.78 ERA. In this project, I grouped five of Logan Webb's worst starts against the five starts he made in June. I examined pitch usage, pitch effectiveness, pitch characteristics, pitch location, count tendencies, and mechanics across the two groups. The goal was to uncover an actionable insight by identifying a changeable difference that could explain Webb's performance. All notebooks, data, and figures used in this analysis are included in the repository and can be explored above.

## Results

Despite the large variation in performance, this analysis did not identify a meaningful adjustment Webb could make. This strongly suggests his performance was driven primarily by short-term volatility rather than a flaw in his approach. While this is a negative result, it remains an important one. Webb has been a longtime ace for the Giants, and when a pitcher of his caliber experiences a stretch like this, it is worth running a diagnostic to see if there is a subtle difference giving hitters an edge. Equally important, this project demonstrates that not every analysis will produce an actionable finding. Forcing a conclusion when the data does not support one is just as misleading as overlooking a real issue. I expect Webb to return to his ace status in the second half, continuing to eat more innings than any pitcher in baseball. As I sit in front of my TV writing this, Webb is six innings into his start against the Mariners: 71 pitches, 0 runs, 1 hit.

## Pitch Usage

Out of all the potential reasons for Webb's poor starts, pitch usage provided the clearest difference between his good and bad starts. In Webb's good starts, four of his five pitches accounted for at least 16% of his total usage, while no pitch exceeded 29%. This forced hitters to respect his entire arsenal and made it difficult to anticipate what was coming next. In his bad starts, he upped his sinker usage from 23% to 37% while phasing out the changeup and four-seam fastball. However, I believe this was a result of his poor performance rather than the cause of it.

## Pitch Effectiveness

All five of Webb's pitches were far less effective in his bad starts. Each pitch saw its OPS increase by between .440 and .885. Rather than providing an insight on its own, this serves as a guide for where to look next. In this case, it showed Webb's struggles were consistent across his entire arsenal rather than isolated to one or two pitches. Webb's whiff rates did not show any dramatic change that helped explain his performance.

## Pitch Characteristics

Webb's velocity, break, and release point were nearly identical between his good and bad starts. Webb's spin rate was slightly lower in his bad starts, but the differences were small relative to the standard deviation of each pitch. If anything, the small decrease could simply reflect fatigue from longer innings rather than a meaningful change in pitch quality.

## Pitch Location & Counts

Webb's pitch location by pitch type was fairly consistent across his good and bad starts. His first-pitch strike rate and general count tendencies were also very similar between the two groups. This indicates there was no deliberate change in his approach.

## Mechanics

Webb's tempo, landing point, hip loading, and poise all remained consistent between his good and bad starts. His release point and arm slot also remained consistent, as shown in the pitch characteristics section. No obvious mechanical explanation emerged from the video review.
